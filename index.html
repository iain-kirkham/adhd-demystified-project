What is ADHD?

ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects attention, impulse control and activity levels. It can present differently between people some have primarily inattentive symptoms (difficulty concentrating, forgetfulness), others more hyperactive/impulsive symptoms (restlessness, interrupting), and many experience a mix (combined). ADHD can affect the mental health of individuals, and can be managed effectively with the right supports.

This site provides practical strategies, links to resources, and information about diagnosis and treatment options. If you think you might have ADHD, consider discussing it with your GP or a specialist.

ADHD types

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects attention, activity levels and impulse control. It commonly presents as predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive/impulsive, or a combined type each with different patterns and practical supports.

Hyperactive / Impulsive symptoms

Hyperactive/impulsive presentation involves high activity levels, restlessness and acting quickly without waiting. People may struggle to sit still, interrupt, or feel constantly "on the go." Movement breaks, clear routines and task segmentation can help manage symptoms. For diagnostic criteria (DSM-5): children and adolescents (under 17) typically meet criteria with 6 or more hyperactive/impulsive symptoms; adults (17+) require 5 or more. Symptoms should have begun before age 12 and cause impairment in two or more settings (for example home and school/work).

Often fidgets with or taps hands or feet, or squirms in seat.

Often leaves seat in situations when remaining seated is expected.

Often runs about or climbs in situations where it is not appropriate (adolescents or adults may be limited to feeling restless).

Often unable to play or take part in leisure activities quietly.

Is often “on the go” acting as if “driven by a motor”.

Often talks excessively.

Often blurts out an answer before a question has been completed.

Often has trouble waiting their turn.

Often interrupts or intrudes on others (e.g. butts into conversations or game)

Inattentive symptoms

Inattentive presentation is marked by difficulty sustaining focus, forgetfulness, and challenges organising tasks. Tasks requiring prolonged mental effort may be especially hard. External organisers, chunking tasks and minimizing distractions are useful supports. For diagnostic criteria (DSM-5): children and adolescents (under 17) typically meet criteria with 6 or more inattentive symptoms; adults (17+) require 5 or more. Symptoms should have begun before age 12 and cause impairment in two or more settings.

Often fails to give close attention to details or makes careless mistakes in schoolwork, at work, or with other activities.

Often has trouble holding attention on tasks or play activities.

Often does not seem to listen when spoken to directly.

Often does not follow through on instructions and fails to finish schoolwork, chores, or duties in the workplace (e.g., loses focus, side-tracked).

Often has trouble organising tasks and activities.

Often avoids, dislikes, or is reluctant to do tasks that require mental effort over a long period of time (such as schoolwork or homework).

Often loses things necessary for tasks and activities (e.g. school materials, pencils, books, tools, wallets, keys, paperwork, eyeglasses, mobile telephones).

Hyperactive / Impulsive symptoms

The combined presentation includes both inattentive and hyperactive/impulsive symptoms. Many people experience a mix — for example, struggling to concentrate while also feeling restless. Management commonly blends structure, behavioural strategies and clinical supports tailored to individual needs. A combined diagnosis requires meeting the symptom-count criteria for both domains (5 of both inattentive and hyperactive/impulsive symptoms in adults and 6 in children).

Diagnosis

Think you might have ADHD? Find out how to get a diagnosis.

How ADHD is diagnosed

Diagnosis combines a clinical interview, validated rating scales, and clear evidence that symptoms affect daily life in multiple settings (home, work, school). For adults, clinicians will also review childhood history and current functioning. Bringing concrete examples, timelines and any previous reports helps the assessor.

NHS guidance on diagnosis

Choose a pathway (NHS, private or RTC)

Options include NHS referral, private assessment, or self- managed supports. In England, some patients can use "Right to Choose" (RTC) to select an approved external provider instead of waiting for local NHS services. RTC availability and eligibility vary depending on location.

Compare pathways

Get assessed

Begin with your GP: describe concerns, give examples of how symptoms affect daily life, and request referral options. If NHS waiting lists are long, ask your GP about RTC (Right to Choose) in England or about accredited private services, be clear on shared care agreements and what documentation the assessor needs.

Where to start

Managing ADHD

Practical strategies: use routines and timers, break tasks into short focused sprints (e.g. Pomodoro), externalise memory with calendars and lists, reduce distractions, prioritise sleep and exercise, and seek therapy or coaching for skills training. Discuss medication options with a clinician where appropriate.
